The Problem

We found that travel outside the Earth's atmosphere, outside the magnetic field and the Earth's gravitational field can harm human health, and understanding this damage is necessary for the success and safety of manned spaceflight. The potential effects on the central nervous system are of particular interest, and cellular and in vivo model research programs have been able to identify the risks to the central nervous system due to cosmic radiation in future long-distance space missions, and promote the development of preventive measures to prevent them. The immediate and delayed risks are The potential on the central nervous system from exposure to cosmic rays and solar protons is a concern for human exploration plans for the solar system. Low and moderate ionizing radiation doses of 0 - 2 Gy (1 Gy = 100 rads) do not pose a real risk.On the central nervous system in adults, however, the heavy ionic component of space radiation presents particular biophysical challenges to cells and tissues compared to the effects caused by terrestrial forms of radiation. Interest in the dangers of cosmic rays began shortly after their discovery, especially with the phenomenon of flashThe optical flicker of the retina confirmed by the Apollo project astronauts between the years (1970-1973), as these rays were able to cause a column of damaged cells along the path of their penetration through the tissues, which raised concerns about the dangerous effects of these rays on the central nervous system.. Other concerns arose with the discovery of neuronal activation by heavy nuclei and its possible effects on the central nervous system. A NASA-supported study also reported that radiation could damage the brain of astronauts and accelerate the onset of Alzheimer's disease. Solar flares (although rare) can cause a fatal dose of radiation within minutes. It is believed that shields and preventive medications may eventually reduce the risk to an acceptable level.
